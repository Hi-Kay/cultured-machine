Oncology June 1, 2026

Moderna and Merck's personalized mRNA cancer vaccine holds up at five years — halving the risk of melanoma recurrence

Melanoma is a dangerous skin cancer that often comes back even after it has been surgically removed. Moderna and Merck have spent years testing a vaccine designed to stop that from happening — and new five-year results, presented at the ASCO cancer conference and published in the Journal of Clinical Oncology, are the strongest yet. Patients who got the vaccine plus Keytruda (a standard immunotherapy) were about half as likely to have their cancer return — or to die — as patients who got Keytruda alone. Five years on, 92% of the vaccine group were still alive, versus 71% of the comparison group.

What makes the vaccine unusual is that it is different for every patient. A COVID vaccine gives everyone the same instructions; here, doctors sequence each person's tumour and a computer algorithm picks out the mutations most likely to catch the immune system's attention — the neoantigens. Those targets are written into a personalised mRNA shot that trains the patient's own immune system to hunt down their specific cancer. That target-picking step — choosing the best few mutations out of hundreds — is where AI quietly does the heavy lifting.

Why it matters: Five years is the point where oncologists start using the word "cure." Most personalised-vaccine signals fade over time; this one held or grew stronger. The honest caveat: this was still a mid-stage trial of just 157 patients, and the larger, definitive Phase 3 trials are still running. But it is the best evidence so far that the approach is worth finishing — and a sign that AI-guided target selection can translate into real survival gains.

Sources: Merck press release · BioSpace / ASCO · NPR

Funding June 2, 2026

A longevity startup raised $435M to reverse cell age — and it's heading to human trials next year

NewLimit, co-founded by Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong, bioengineer Blake Byers, and CEO Jacob Kimmel, closed a $435 million Series C led by Founders Fund, valuing the company at roughly $3.1 billion. The company plans to take its first drug — targeting fatty liver disease — into human trials in 2027. Its approach is cellular reprogramming: using combinations of transcription factors (proteins that switch genes on and off) to reset a cell's biological "age state" without tipping it into becoming cancerous. A proprietary AI model named Ambrosia sifts through the huge number of possible factor combinations to find promising ones, and the team reports reversing signs of ageing in old human liver cells in the lab.

Why it matters: Cellular reprogramming is the most ambitious idea in longevity science — actually resetting a cell's molecular clock rather than just slowing it down. It has failed in cells and animals before, so scepticism is warranted. What's notable here is that NewLimit is among the first companies to move past published experiments toward an IND application — the FDA filing a drug needs before it can be tested in people. The open question is whether those promising lab results will hold up once the drug reaches real patients.

Sources: STAT News · NewLimit blog

Funding June 11, 2026

Jeff Bezos raises $12 billion to build an "artificial general engineer" — including for drug design

Prometheus, a physical-AI startup co-founded by Jeff Bezos and Vik Bajaj (co-founder of Alphabet's Verily life-sciences unit), raised $12 billion at a $41 billion valuation — one of the largest AI funding rounds ever. Backers include JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and BlackRock. Prometheus's pitch is an "artificial general engineer": AI that designs complex physical systems autonomously, from jet engines to drug molecules. Bajaj brings direct biopharma experience from Verily and from earlier work at academic medical centers; the company says life sciences is one of its primary target domains alongside materials and energy.

Why it matters: At $41 billion pre-revenue, this is a bet that AI's next expansion is from text and images into the physical and chemical world. For biotech specifically, the Bajaj angle matters — this is not a software lab dabbling in biology, but a founder with deep domain experience. Whether "artificial general engineer" becomes a real capability or just a well-funded slogan will be legible within a few years.

Sources: TechCrunch · STAT

Foundation models June 16, 2026

A failed ALS trial gave birth to a brain foundation model — one that reads your brain's molecular state from a blood draw

Verge Labs (formerly Verge Genomics) released vBx-1.0, which it calls a foundation model for the brain. The idea is striking: from an ordinary blood draw, the model reconstructs what is happening at the molecular level inside a patient's brain — and predicts how diseases like Parkinson's, ALS, and Alzheimer's will progress and respond to treatment. It was trained on brain gene-activity data from 6,500 patients, matched to their blood samples and medical records, and the company reports up to 3× the accuracy of the previous best methods. The backstory is unusual: Verge's own experimental ALS drug failed an early trial last month, with a third of patients dropping out because they couldn't tolerate it. Instead of quietly moving on, the team published a detailed account of what went wrong and turned the underlying biological data into the product.

Why it matters: Brain-disease trials fail at very high rates, partly because patients who look similar can be biologically very different — so a drug that helps a subset gets buried in the average. A non-invasive tool that predicts who will respond to what, if it holds up on independent data, could change how those trials are designed. The honesty is notable too: publishing exactly why a trial failed is rarer in biotech than it should be.

Sources: STAT News · PR Newswire / Verge Labs

Reality check June 15, 2026

Precision psychiatry, 0 for 3: Neumora's depression drug fails its final Phase 3 trial and lays off a third of its staff

Neumora Therapeutics reported that navacaprant failed to outperform placebo in two Phase 3 trials — KOASTAL-2 and KOASTAL-3 — making three consecutive Phase 3 failures for the drug. Navacaprant is a receptor antagonist targeting the kappa opioid receptor, a protein in the brain thought to regulate mood and contribute to depressive symptoms when overactive. Despite a clean safety profile, patients who received navacaprant showed no statistically significant improvement on the MADRS scale — the standard 10-item clinician rating used to measure depression severity — compared to patients who received an inactive treatment. Neumora, backed by ARCH Venture Partners and Amgen, will cut 35% of its workforce and refocus on earlier-stage programs in Alzheimer's agitation and schizophrenia.

Why it matters: Depression is one of the most-funded and most clinically difficult areas of drug development. Navacaprant illustrates a recurring pattern: a biologically plausible target, a well-characterized drug molecule, a clean safety profile — and still no detectable benefit in a large controlled trial. Several factors can produce this outcome: placebo response rates in depression trials are consistently high (often 35–45%), enrolled patients are rarely biologically homogeneous, and standard rating scales may not be sensitive enough to detect a real but modest effect. This is not evidence that targeting the kappa opioid receptor is wrong in principle, but a reminder that clinical validation requires more than a credible mechanism.

Sources: Endpoints News · Fierce Biotech
