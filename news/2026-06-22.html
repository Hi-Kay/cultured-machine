Oncology June 1, 2026

Moderna and Merck's personalized mRNA cancer vaccine holds up at five years — halving the risk of melanoma recurrence

Five-year follow-up data from the Phase 2b KEYNOTE-942 trial, presented at ASCO 2026 and simultaneously published in the Journal of Clinical Oncology, showed that patients with high-risk resected melanoma who received Moderna and Merck's personalized mRNA vaccine (intismeran autogene, formerly mRNA-4157) alongside pembrolizumab (Keytruda) cut their risk of recurrence or death by 49% versus Keytruda alone. Five-year overall survival rates were 92.2% in the combination arm versus 71.3% for immunotherapy alone — a striking gap for a disease where late recurrences are common. The vaccine is manufactured individually for each patient by sequencing their own tumor's mutations and encoding up to 34 of the resulting neoantigens in a single mRNA shot.

Why it matters: Five years is the threshold oncologists use to start talking about cure. Most personalized vaccine signals fade over time — these held or widened. The catch: this is still a Phase 2b trial with 157 patients, and Phase 3 trials in melanoma and other solid tumors are still running. These results are the strongest clinical argument yet that they're worth finishing.

Sources: Merck press release · BioSpace / ASCO · NPR

Funding June 2, 2026

A longevity startup raised $435M to reverse cell age — and it's heading to human trials next year

NewLimit, co-founded by Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong, bioengineer Blake Byers, and CEO Jacob Kimmel, closed a $435 million Series C led by Founders Fund, valuing the company at roughly $3.1 billion. The capital will take its first drug — targeting fatty liver disease — into a clinical trial in 2027. NewLimit's approach is cellular reprogramming: using combinations of transcription factors (proteins that switch genes on and off) to reset a cell's epigenetic "age state" without the risk of turning it cancerous. Its proprietary AI model, Ambrosia, screens those combinations; the team reports seeing age reversal in old human liver cells in the lab.

Why it matters: Cellular reprogramming is the most ambitious idea in longevity science — resetting the molecular clock of a specific cell type rather than just slowing it. It has failed in cells and animal models before. NewLimit is among the first companies to move past publications and toward an IND application. Whether the liver effects survive contact with a clinical trial is the $435 million question.

Sources: STAT News · NewLimit blog

Funding June 11, 2026

Jeff Bezos raises $12 billion to build an "artificial general engineer" — including for drug design

Prometheus, a physical-AI startup co-founded by Jeff Bezos and Vik Bajaj (co-founder of Alphabet's Verily life-sciences unit), raised $12 billion at a $41 billion valuation — one of the largest AI funding rounds ever. Backers include JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and BlackRock. Prometheus's pitch is an "artificial general engineer": AI that designs complex physical systems autonomously, from jet engines to drug molecules. Bajaj brings direct biopharma experience from Verily and from earlier work at academic medical centers; the company says life sciences is one of its primary target domains alongside materials and energy.

Why it matters: At $41 billion pre-revenue, this is a bet that AI's next expansion is from text and images into the physical and chemical world. For biotech specifically, the Bajaj angle matters — this is not a software lab dabbling in biology, but a founder with deep domain experience. Whether "artificial general engineer" becomes a real capability or just a well-funded slogan will be legible within a few years.

Sources: TechCrunch · STAT

Foundation models June 16, 2026

A failed ALS trial gave birth to a brain foundation model — one that reads your brain's molecular state from a blood draw

Verge Labs (formerly Verge Genomics) launched vBx-1.0 on June 16, a foundation model for precision neurology trained on over 12,000 brain transcriptomes from 6,500 patients, matched to blood proteomics, genomics, and clinical data. From a routine blood draw, vBx-1.0 reconstructs the molecular state of a patient's brain with up to 3× the accuracy of the previous state of the art, and predicts disease progression and treatment response across Parkinson's, ALS, and Alzheimer's. The origin story: Verge's own ALS drug failed a Phase 1b trial last month, with a third of patients dropping out due to tolerability issues. Rather than folding, the team published a detailed post-mortem and repositioned the biological data as the core asset.

Why it matters: Neurology clinical trials fail at extraordinarily high rates, in part because patient populations are far more heterogeneous than they appear. A non-invasive tool that predicts who responds to what — if validated on independent datasets — would change how trials are designed. The honest accounting of the trial failure is also notable: Verge published what went wrong, which is rarer in biotech than it should be.

Sources: STAT News · PR Newswire / Verge Labs

Regulation June 15, 2026

Medicare will cover Wegovy and Zepbound for $50 a month starting July 1 — but the program is already on shaky ground

CMS launched the "Medicare GLP-1 Bridge," a temporary coverage program running from July 1, 2026 through end of 2027, under which eligible Medicare beneficiaries (BMI ≥ 27 plus an obesity-related condition, or BMI ≥ 35) can access semaglutide (Wegovy) or tirzepatide (Zepbound) at a $50 monthly copay. The arrangement came after private Medicare Advantage insurers refused to participate in the administration's original voluntary BALANCE coverage model, citing unquantifiable costs — so the federal government extended a transitional program and will cover costs directly from taxpayers. CMS has not published a cost estimate for the program.

Why it matters: GLP-1 drugs are extraordinarily effective at reducing obesity-related disease burden — but stopping them reverses the effects rapidly, making continuity of coverage a clinical necessity, not a convenience. A "temporary" program that covers millions of patients and then sunsets is a policy contradiction in progress. The market signal for Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly is large and unambiguous; the budget math is still missing.

Sources: STAT News · CMS · KFF

Reality check June 15, 2026

Precision psychiatry, 0 for 3: Neumora's depression drug fails its final Phase 3 trial and lays off a third of its staff

Neumora Therapeutics reported that navacaprant failed to outperform placebo in two Phase 3 trials — KOASTAL-2 and KOASTAL-3 — making three consecutive Phase 3 failures for the drug. Navacaprant is a receptor antagonist targeting the kappa opioid receptor, a protein in the brain thought to regulate mood and contribute to depressive symptoms when overactive. Despite a clean safety profile, patients who received navacaprant showed no statistically significant improvement on the MADRS scale — the standard 10-item clinician rating used to measure depression severity — compared to patients who received an inactive treatment. Neumora, backed by ARCH Venture Partners and Amgen, will cut 35% of its workforce and refocus on earlier-stage programs in Alzheimer's agitation and schizophrenia.

Why it matters: Depression is one of the most-funded and most clinically difficult areas of drug development. Navacaprant illustrates a recurring pattern: a biologically plausible target, a well-characterized drug molecule, a clean safety profile — and still no detectable benefit in a large controlled trial. Several factors can produce this outcome: placebo response rates in depression trials are consistently high (often 35–45%), enrolled patients are rarely biologically homogeneous, and standard rating scales may not be sensitive enough to detect a real but modest effect. This is not evidence that targeting the kappa opioid receptor is wrong in principle, but a reminder that clinical validation requires more than a credible mechanism.

Sources: Endpoints News · Fierce Biotech
