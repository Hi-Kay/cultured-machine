Drug discovery June 5, 2026

An AI designed a "universal" coronavirus vaccine from scratch — and it just passed its first human trial

Researchers at the University of Cambridge and its spinout DIOSynVax used AI and machine learning to build a single vaccine antigen that targets features shared across the entire Sarbecovirus family — the group that includes SARS-CoV-2, original SARS, and related bat coronaviruses with pandemic potential. The antigen (called a "super-antigen") was designed entirely in silico: no wet lab tinkering of a known strain, just pattern-recognition across global virus surveillance data. A Phase 1 trial in 39 healthy volunteers found the vaccine safe, well-tolerated, and capable of generating immune responses against multiple coronaviruses simultaneously — including bat viruses that haven't yet infected humans. The trial used needle-free delivery via a micro fluid jet; results were published in the Journal of Infection.

Why it matters: This is the first time a vaccine whose active ingredient was generated entirely by computer has cleared a human safety trial — a milestone separate from its efficacy. If Phase 2 confirms broad protection, the same design logic could be applied to other virus families (Ebola, influenza) and could eventually eliminate the need to reformulate vaccines every time a new variant emerges.

Sources: ScienceDaily / University of Cambridge · Medical Xpress

Oncology June 10, 2026

Ozempic linked to 30–47% lower breast cancer risk — and weight loss may not be why

A large real-world study presented at ASCO 2026 (the American Society of Clinical Oncology annual meeting) found that women with type 2 diabetes or obesity who used GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) or tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) — had a 30–47% lower risk of developing breast cancer compared to non-users. The striking part: the protective signal held up even after researchers controlled for body mass index and weight change, suggesting that something beyond simple fat-mass reduction is at work. Proposed mechanisms include reduced insulin-like growth factor (IGF-1) signalling, anti-inflammatory effects, and possible direct activity of GLP-1 receptors on breast tissue.

Why it matters: This is observational data, not a randomised trial — so causation isn't proven yet. But with ~15 million Americans already on GLP-1 drugs, an independently acting cancer-protective effect would be a major public health development. A prospective trial (the ORCA study) is under way. Watch for those results before drawing strong conclusions.

Sources: Medical Daily

Drug discovery June 4–5, 2026

Recursion's AI-identified cancer drug clears its first human safety bar — a small but necessary step

At the Goldman Sachs Global Healthcare Conference, Recursion Pharmaceuticals reported early Phase 1 data for REC-1245 — a first-in-class degrader of RBM39 (RNA Binding Motif Protein 39), an oncology target Recursion's platform identified by mining its massive cellular imaging library. In 16 patients with treatment-resistant solid tumors or lymphoma, no dose-limiting toxicities were observed — the primary safety hurdle for a Phase 1 trial. Recursion's stock rose ~5% on the update. A parallel program, REC-4881 for a rare blood disorder, is also in the clinic.

Why it matters: Recursion's entire investment thesis depends on its AI finding real targets that survive the jump into humans — not just computational predictions that fail on contact with biology. No DLTs is the lowest bar, but it's a bar. The field needs to see these AI-origin programs progress through Phase 2 before the platform claim holds.

Sources: GEN · Recursion IR

Funding June 2026

Lila Sciences raises $350M to build AI that runs the scientific method — autonomously, around the clock

Lila Sciences, a Flagship Pioneering spinout co-founded by Noubar Afeyan (also the co-founder of Moderna) and CEO Geoffrey von Maltzahn, closed a $350 million Series A — bringing total funding to $550 million. NVIDIA's venture arm NVentures joined the round, alongside General Catalyst, ARK Venture Fund, and Abu Dhabi's ADIA. Lila's model: AI agents that run the full scientific loop (hypothesis → experiment → observation → reasoning) continuously across materials, chemistry, and life sciences. An early demonstration: Lila's agents identified water-splitting catalysts that outperform precious metals — and a third of the highest-performing candidates initially made no sense to the Caltech-trained team. They turned out to be the best catalysts on record.

Why it matters: This is the step beyond AlphaFold. Structure prediction was act one; autonomous AI-driven experimentation across disciplines is act two. The NVIDIA investment is a tell — they're betting that foundation models and lab-in-the-loop AI will converge, and that Lila is building the infrastructure to run that future.

Sources: Finsmes · Fortune Brainstorm Tech

Reality check June 10, 2026

"Defunding science right now is an unimaginable strategic mistake." AI biotech leaders speak out at Fortune Brainstorm Tech

At Fortune's Brainstorm Tech conference in Aspen, Lila Sciences CEO Geoffrey von Maltzahn and NVIDIA VP of Healthcare Kimberly Powell warned that US government cuts to health research funding — arriving just as AI is having its "ChatGPT moment" in biology — risk ceding ground to Europe and Asia. Powell: "If we defund now, while the rest of the world leans in — which Europe is leaning in, which a lot of Asian countries are leaning in — we will be left behind." Von Maltzahn: "Falling below the scientific intelligence of one's adversary is almost like an unimaginable competitive disadvantage." The AI drug discovery market currently stands at $3.25 billion and is growing at ~26% annually.

Why it matters: Open-source foundation models for biology — the ones companies like Recursion, Isomorphic, and Lila build on — depend heavily on public science infrastructure: NIH-funded datasets, academic training runs, university spinouts. Defund the base layer and the private stack slows down too. This is a longer-term threat than it looks.

Sources: Fortune

Regulation June 2026

AI is moving faster in healthcare than trust is. The Philips Future Health Index 2026 puts numbers on the gap.

Philips' annual Future Health Index surveyed healthcare leaders globally and found that while AI adoption in clinical care is accelerating, a majority of doctors and patients report significant trust gaps: they describe unclear AI decision-making, inadequate training, and anxiety about AI errors. The report finds AI outpacing the governance, communication, and workflow integration needed to make it safe in practice — creating a scenario where clinicians either over-rely on AI outputs or route around them entirely.

Why it matters: A diagnostic AI that clinicians don't trust doesn't get used. One they trust blindly causes harm. Getting adoption right requires the unglamorous work of explainability, interface design, and institutional governance — none of which shows up in benchmark papers. The report is a useful reality check on the gap between "we deployed an AI" and "patients benefit from it."

Sources: Medical Daily / Philips Future Health Index 2026
