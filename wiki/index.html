Neuroscience

Kappa opioid receptor (KOR)

A protein found on the surface of nerve cells, mainly in the brain and spinal cord. It belongs to the opioid receptor family — the same family targeted by morphine and related pain drugs, though KOR is a distinct subtype with different effects. When activated by the body's natural signalling molecules (called dynorphins), the kappa opioid receptor produces effects including reduced pain sensation, sedation, and — at high activation levels — depressed mood, anxiety, and feelings of disconnection. This link to negative emotional states is the reason the KOR has been studied as a drug target in major depressive disorder. A drug that blocks this receptor (a kappa opioid receptor antagonist) is hypothesised to counteract these negative mood effects.

Clinical measurement

MADRS — Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale

A standardised questionnaire completed by a clinician (not the patient) to measure the severity of depression. It has 10 items — including apparent sadness, reported sadness, inner tension, reduced sleep, reduced appetite, difficulty concentrating, fatigue, inability to feel, pessimistic thoughts, and thoughts of suicide — each scored from 0 to 6. The total score ranges from 0 (no depression) to 60 (severe depression). In clinical trials, a reduction of 50% or more from baseline is typically used to define a "response," and a score below 10 is often used to define remission. The MADRS is one of the most widely used outcome measures in antidepressant trials; it was developed to be sensitive to change, making it useful for detecting drug effects.

Psychiatry

Major depressive disorder (MDD)

A medical diagnosis characterised by persistent low mood or loss of interest in activities, lasting at least two weeks and severe enough to interfere with daily life. Other common symptoms include changes in sleep, appetite, or energy; difficulty concentrating; feelings of worthlessness or guilt; and, in severe cases, thoughts of suicide. MDD is distinct from normal sadness or grief in its duration, severity, and functional impact. It affects approximately 280 million people worldwide and is one of the leading causes of disability globally. Developing effective treatments for MDD is challenging: the biological causes vary considerably between patients, placebo response rates in trials are high, and it is difficult to select the right patient population for a given drug mechanism.

Molecular biology

mRNA (messenger RNA)

A molecule that carries genetic instructions from DNA to the protein-making machinery inside a cell. DNA in the cell nucleus stores the blueprint for every protein the body makes; mRNA is a temporary copy of one specific instruction set, read out and translated into protein before being broken down. In medicine, synthetic mRNA can be delivered into cells to instruct them to produce a specific protein — for example, an antigen (a fragment of a pathogen) that the immune system can learn to recognise. This is how mRNA vaccines work: the injected mRNA causes the body's own cells to briefly produce a viral protein, training the immune system without ever introducing the actual virus. Personalised cancer vaccines extend this logic: the mRNA is custom-designed for each patient using the unique mutations in their tumour, instructing the immune system to recognise and attack cells carrying those mutations.

Clinical research

Phase 1, Phase 2, and Phase 3 clinical trials

The three main stages of human testing required before a drug can receive regulatory approval. Phase 1 trials are the first time a drug is tested in humans; they typically enrol 20–100 healthy volunteers or patients and focus on safety — what doses are tolerable and how the body processes the drug. Phase 2 trials expand to hundreds of patients, continuing to assess safety but also gathering early evidence of whether the drug works. Phase 3 trials are large, typically randomised and placebo-controlled, enrolling hundreds to thousands of patients at multiple sites; they are designed to definitively confirm or refute efficacy and to detect less common side effects. Passing all three phases is required before regulatory submission. Phase 1b is a sub-stage of Phase 1, often a dose-expansion study in a more targeted patient group after initial safety is established.

Clinical research

Placebo response

Improvement in symptoms that occurs in clinical trial participants who receive an inactive treatment (the placebo) rather than the drug being tested. Placebo responses are a genuine biological phenomenon, not simply patients pretending to feel better: expectation, attention from medical staff, natural fluctuation in disease severity, and regression to the mean (the tendency of symptoms that were unusually bad at enrolment to improve on their own) all contribute. In depression trials specifically, placebo response rates are consistently high — often 35–45% of patients improve substantially on placebo alone. This makes it harder to demonstrate that an active drug does anything beyond what a placebo would achieve, because the difference between the two groups (the "drug effect") must reach statistical significance to count as evidence of efficacy.

Pharmacology

Receptor antagonist

A drug molecule that binds to a specific receptor — typically a protein on the surface or inside a cell — and blocks it from being activated by its natural trigger. Receptors are the "docking stations" that cells use to receive chemical signals; the body's own signalling molecules (hormones, neurotransmitters) bind to them and trigger a response. An antagonist occupies the same binding site without activating it, effectively switching the receptor off. This is different from an agonist, which binds and activates the receptor, mimicking the natural signal. Many widely used drugs are antagonists: beta-blockers (used in heart disease) block adrenaline receptors; antihistamines block histamine receptors to reduce allergy symptoms. In the context of the kappa opioid receptor, an antagonist blocks a receptor linked to negative mood states, with the aim of reducing depressive symptoms.
